Boosting vs normalizing — what's the difference?

Boosting adds a fixed decibel amount to every sample of your audio. If you choose +6 dB, every quiet recording goes up by exactly 6 dB regardless of how loud it already was. This is the right choice when you already know your file is too quiet and you want a simple bump up — for example, a phone voice memo or a soft microphone recording.

Normalizing first scans the entire clip to find its single loudest peak, then scales the whole file by exactly the amount needed to make that peak hit a target you choose (typically -1 dB or -3 dB to leave a safety margin before clipping). The result: loud files stay loud, quiet files come up to a comparable level — all without clipping by design. Pick normalize when you have several files of different loudness and want a roughly consistent playback level, or when you don't know how loud your input is.

Both modes run entirely in your browser via the Web Audio API: the file is decoded into a raw PCM buffer, a gain node multiplies every sample by the chosen factor, and the buffer is then re-encoded back to MP3 (192 kbps via lamejs) or WAV (lossless PCM). Nothing is uploaded anywhere — your audio stays on your own device the whole time.

Set expectations right

Boost can clip: samples above 0 dB get clipped (the card warns you).
Normalize never clips: your target peak caps the output by design.
MP3 re-encodes: small loss of quality on the path through MP3 (192 kbps is fine for voice).
WAV preserves: the boosted buffer stores as lossless PCM if you need it pristine.

Why boost your audio in the browser instead of uploading it to a website?

Most online audio volume boosters accept your file, upload it to a server, run a backend library such as FFmpeg or SoX to apply the gain, then send the boosted file back. Browser-side conversion keeps the entire pipeline in your tab: your file is decoded locally by the Web Audio API, every sample is multiplied by the gain factor in JavaScript, and the result is re-encoded back to MP3 or WAV — all without the file ever leaving your device. For sensitive audio (voice memos with names, podcast drafts, unreleased music), being able to do that entirely offline is the safer choice.
